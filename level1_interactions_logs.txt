Step 4: When sunlight passes through a raindrop, it is bent (refracted) and separated into its constituent colors.

Step 5:  These colors are then reflected internally within the raindrop before being refracted again as they exit, resulting in the visible spectrum we see.  The order of the colors is consistent.

Step 6: The colors of the rainbow, in order from the top (usually outermost) to the bottom (usually innermost), are red, orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo, and violet.

You: which is the hottest planet?
Assistant: Step 1:  We need to define "hottest."  Are we talking about surface temperature, or average temperature throughout the planet's atmosphere?  Surface temperature is typically what people mean when they ask this question.

Step 2:  Venus and Mercury are often contenders for the title of "hottest planet."  Both are relatively close to the Sun.

Step 3: While Mercury is closer to the Sun, it lacks a significant atmosphere to trap heat.

Step 4: Venus, however, possesses a very thick atmosphere composed primarily of carbon dioxide. This creates a powerful greenhouse effect.

Step 5: This greenhouse effect traps solar radiation, leading to incredibly high surface temperatures on Venus.

Step 6: Therefore, despite being farther from the Sun than Mercury, Venus has a much hotter surface temperature and is considered the hottest planet in our solar system.

You: Tell me why sky is blue?
Assistant: Step 1: Sunlight, which appears white to us, is actually made up of all the colors of the rainbow.  Think of a prism separating white light into its constituent colors.

Step 2: As sunlight enters the Earth's atmosphere, it collides with tiny air molecules (mostly nitrogen and oxygen).

Step 3: This collision causes a phenomenon called scattering.  Scattering is the redirection of light in different directions.

Step 4:  Shorter wavelengths of light, like blue and violet, are scattered much more efficiently than longer wavelengths, like red and orange. This is because of a physics principle called Rayleigh scattering.

Step 5:  Therefore, the blue light is scattered in all directions, making the sky appear blue to our eyes.  While violet light is scattered even more, our eyes are less sensitive to violet, and the sun emits slightly less violet light, resulting in a blue sky.
